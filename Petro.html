Angono-Binangonan Petroglyphs

Historical Background

The Angono-Binangonan Petroglyphs stand as enduring relics of prehistoric art, tracing back to the late Neolithic period around 2000 B.C. This era represents a pivotal time in human history when artistic expression began to intertwine with evolving societal structures. The discovery of these petroglyphs by National Artist Carlos "Botong" Francisco in 1965 sparked a renewed interest in understanding the ancient cultures that once thrived in the Philippines.

Philosophy

Embedded within the intricate carvings of the petroglyphs is a profound philosophy that echoes the deep-rooted connection between early inhabitants and the natural world. These engravings serve as visual narratives, weaving together stories of myth, ritual, and daily life experiences. Moreover, the inclusion of anthropomorphic figures with symbolic features hints at a spiritual philosophy, suggesting a reverence for deities or ancestral spirits.

Characteristics

The petroglyphs exhibit a striking blend of geometric patterns and human-like figures, showcasing the artistic versatility and ingenuity of ancient Filipino artisans. Carved into the walls of a shallow cave within volcanic tuff, these artworks seamlessly integrate with their natural surroundings. Despite the passage of millennia, the petroglyphs retain their intricate details, such as digits and female genitalia on certain figures, highlighting the skill and craftsmanship of their creators.
