Planting Rice

Fernando Amorsolo

Fernando Amorsolo

His rural landscapes, often bathed in warm sunlight, became emblematic of Filipino life and culture. In 1947, he completed the famous painting “Planting Rice”, showcasing his love for Filipino traditions and way of life. Amorsolo’s legacy extends beyond technique; he shaped Filipino notions of self and identity through his art. Philosophy: The painting elicits a sense of communal cohesion and the symbiotic relationship between people and their land. Through vibrant colors and skillful use of light, Amorsolo elevates this everyday scene to a celebration of Filipino identity. The sensualized depictions of women farmers convey a deeper connection between the land, its people, and their shared existence.

Historical Background

Amorsolo's rice cycle paintings predominantly feature women, highlighting their crucial but often overlooked role in rice production in Central Luzon, Philippines, known as the "Rice Granary." Amorsolo's genre paintings reflect his deep understanding of rural traditions, portraying peasants in vibrant rustic attire with contented expressions against bright backgrounds, symbolizing the significance of rice in both sustenance and cultural identity.

Characteristics

Amorsolo used oil paints on canvas to create this artwork. Measuring approximately 95.5 x 131 cm. The size and scale of the painting contribute to its immersive and detailed depiction of a group of farmers planting rice in a lush, green field.The color palette of "Planting Rice" by Fernando Amorsolo consists of various shades of green, brown, yellow, and white. The surface finish of the painting is expertly executed, showcasing Amorsolo's skill as a masterful realist painter.
