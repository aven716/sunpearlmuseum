Cypress Trees

by Kano Eitoku

Kano Eitoku

Kanō Eitoku (born Feb. 16, 1543, Kyōto, Japan—died Oct. 12, 1590, Kyōto) was a fifth-generation scion of the famous Kanō family of Japanese artists who created the style of the Azuchi-Momoyama period (1574–1600) screen paintings.

Historical Background

The folding screen is a valuable example of grand polychrome-and-gold painting from the Momoyama period. It was passed down within the Katsuranomiya family and later designated as a Treasure of the Imperial Collection. Traces of door pulls suggest it was originally part of sliding doors in the Hachijônomiya residence, created around 1590 by Kanô Eitoku.

Philosophy

The screen features an enormous cypress tree against a gold-leafed background with clouds. The artist simplified the background, used minimal colors, and energetically depicted the tree bark. The powerful tree symbolizes life force and reflects the unconstrained aesthetic of Momoyama elite warriors.

Characteristics

“Cypress Trees” is a monumental artwork printed with gold on washi paper. It was designed for four-fold screens, each measuring H170.0 × W230.4 cm. Eitoku’s mastery of traditional Japanese techniques, including gold leaf and washi paper, is evident in this piece, which symbolizes longevity and resilience in nature.
